Hexagonal Architecture, also known as Ports and Adapters Architecture, was created by Alistair Cockburn. The main objective of this architectural pattern is to build systems with low coupling, high cohesion, technology independence, and better maintainability. In Hexagonal Architecture, the business rules remain isolated from external technologies such as databases, frameworks, APIs, and user interfaces.

The architecture is organized around the core domain of the system. Communication between the domain and external systems happens through ports and adapters. Ports are interfaces that define how communication occurs, while adapters implement these interfaces to connect the application with technologies such as REST APIs, databases, or messaging systems. This approach allows infrastructure components to be replaced without changing the business logic.

One of the greatest benefits of Hexagonal Architecture is testability. Since the business logic is independent from frameworks and infrastructure, unit testing becomes simpler and more reliable. Another important benefit is flexibility, because technologies can be changed with minimal impact on the system core. The architecture also improves code organization and reuse, making maintenance easier over time.

Although Hexagonal Architecture and Clean Architecture share similar principles, they are not the same. Both focus on separating business rules from infrastructure and external technologies. However, Clean Architecture organizes the system into concentric layers with strict dependency rules, while Hexagonal Architecture focuses on communication through ports and adapters around the application core. Hexagonal Architecture is generally more focused on interaction between the domain and external systems.

In my opinion, Hexagonal Architecture should not be used in every type of project. Small applications with low complexity and short lifespans may not benefit from the additional structure and development effort required by this pattern. Before deciding to use Hexagonal Architecture, developers should consider factors such as project complexity, expected maintenance, scalability requirements, testing needs, team experience, and long-term evolution of the system. For medium and large systems that require flexibility and maintainability, Hexagonal Architecture can be an excellent choice.
